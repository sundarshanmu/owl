§ 01 · The Thesis

Build the rails, not the ride.

You asked for a long-game play with no fallback option. That rules out the loud, consumer-facing categories (neobanks, BNPL, crypto exchanges) where survival depends on capital cycles and brand wars. The only fintech segments that compound for a decade regardless of the macro are the ones financial institutions are forced to use.

1.

Compliance is non-optional.

Every fintech, bank, and platform offering financial services must do KYC, AML, transaction monitoring, sanctions screening, and regulatory reporting. The US GENIUS Act, EU MiCA & DORA, India's DPDP Act, and the EU AI Act have hard-coded RegTech as core infrastructure from 2025 onward.

2.

The math compounds.

RegTech reduces compliance costs 30–50% for mid-sized fintechs. Pricing is per-transaction and per-API-call, not per-seat — so revenue scales with the customer's growth curve, not their headcount. This is the SaaS economics every category dreams of.

3.

AI gives you a 10-year window.

Legacy RegTech (Actimize, Refinitiv) is built on rules engines from the 2010s. Agentic AI on graph-based transaction data delivers 60–80% lower false-positive rates. Replacement cycle is measured in years, not months — defensible.

4.

You don't need a banking license.

Unlike neobanks or lending, RegTech is sold to financial institutions — they hold the licenses. You stay capital-light, software-margin, and avoid the regulatory capital trap that kills most fintechs in year 5.

§ 03 · Execution

From kitchen-table to ₹100 Cr ARR.

A 30-month plan, broken into four phases. Each phase has a single outcome, a hard go/no-go gate, and a named deliverable. There are no fallback paths — every phase commits to the next.

Phase 0 · Foundation

Month 0–2 · Pre-Code

You don't write a line of product code in this phase. You earn the right to build by buying signal nobody else has.

  • Co-founder pairing: One business/regulatory founder (ex-RBI / ex-Big-4 financial-crime / ex-bank-compliance), one technical founder with distributed-systems and ML background. Solo founders should not start here.
  • Design partner LOIs: Sign 3 paid pilot LOIs from Indian fintechs (one neobank, one lending platform, one PA/PG) at ₹15–25 lakh / 6-month pilot. If you cannot get 3 LOIs in 8 weeks, the thesis is wrong — stop.
  • Entity setup: Pvt Ltd in India + a Delaware C-Corp parent for future global fundraising. Get RBI Regulatory Sandbox application drafted (entry not required, but optionality matters).
  • Capital: Raise a $1.5–2.5M seed from RegTech-literate funds (Better Capital, Blume, BEENEXT, Saison Capital, FinTech Collective).
Phase Gate3 paid pilot contracts signed. Seed closed. Two co-founders aligned in writing on equity, vision, and 5-year commitment.

Phase 1 · Build the Wedge

Month 3–9 · MVP → First ₹

Single product, single workflow, deeply integrated with the three pilot customers. The goal is not features — it's a reduction in their false-positive rate from ~95% to under 30%, measured weekly.

  • Hire: 8 people total. 4 backend engineers, 1 ML engineer, 1 data engineer, 1 compliance solutions architect, 1 designer. No sales hire yet.
  • Build: The graph-AI transaction monitoring engine (see § 05). Every alert ships with an explainability trace good enough to file with FIU-IND.
  • Deploy: On the customer's VPC (private deployment) for the first three. This kills security objections and gives you painful, real-world data.
  • Compliance: ISO 27001 + SOC 2 Type I started in Month 4, finished by Month 9. Non-negotiable.
  • Metric to obsess over: Customer's compliance team time saved per week. Target: 60% by Month 9.
Phase Gate3 pilots converted to annual contracts at ₹35–60 lakh ACV. SOC 2 Type I report. ARR run-rate ₹1.5 Cr+ by Month 9.

Phase 2 · Land & Expand in India

Month 9–18 · Scale to 25 customers

Same product, more customers, deeper inside each one. You are not adding a second product yet. Discipline here is what kills your competitors later.

  • Sales motion: Founder-led + 2 enterprise AEs hired Month 10–12. ICP is fintechs with 500K+ users and at least one regulated entity in the group.
  • Channel: Sponsor & speak at FinTech Convergence Council, Global FinTech Festival (Mumbai), MoneyLIVE. Be the technical voice on AML in India.
  • Customer expansion: Add Sanctions Screening as a paid module. ARR from existing customers should grow 60% on net-revenue-retention.
  • SOC 2 Type II + ISO 27001 certification. Series A enabler.
  • Series A: Raise $10–18M at Month 15–18 once you have ₹15 Cr+ ARR and 25+ paying customers. Lead from Accel / Lightspeed / Elevation / QED.
Phase Gate25 paying customers. ₹18–25 Cr ARR. NRR > 130%. Series A closed.

Month 18–30 · International + Module 2

Two parallel tracks. Track A: Open Dubai office, win 8–12 GCC customers at $150K+ ACV. Track B: Ship Module 2 (Continuous KYC) to your installed base.

  • Dubai entity: Dubai International Financial Centre (DIFC) registration. One AE + one solutions architect on the ground by Month 20.

§ 04 · Technology Stack

Chosen to age well.

Every choice below is optimised for one criterion: will this still be a defensible, hireable, performant decision in 2034? Hype-resistant, vendor-lock-resistant, regulator-defensible.

The architecture is a polyglot, event-sourced, multi-tenant platform with strict deployment isolation per customer (private VPC + shared control plane). This is the only model that satisfies bank/fintech security review while still letting you ship features once and roll them everywhere.

Why these — and why not the obvious alternatives

Why Go + Python + Rust (no Java, no Node)

Java has the ecosystem but the talent pool is exiting the language. Node is fine for CRUD but breaks under sustained low-latency stream loads. Go gives you the deployment simplicity and concurrency model fintech needs; Python is irreplaceable for ML; Rust enters in Phase 2 for the highest-throughput stream paths where every microsecond matters in fraud detection.

Why Kafka + Flink, not Kinesis or managed-only

Kafka is the only event-streaming substrate with a 10-year track record at fintech scale (Robinhood, Stripe, Goldman Sachs). Flink lets you do exactly-once stream processing, which is essential for compliance — a single duplicated alert is a regulatory incident. Avoid AWS Kinesis lock-in; you will need to deploy in customer VPCs eventually.

Why Neo4j (graph-first), not just Postgres

Money-laundering, sanctions evasion, and synthetic-identity fraud are relational problems — they live in the connections between entities, not in the entities themselves. Legacy AML systems use SQL and lose. A graph-native data model gives you 100× better detection on layered fraud rings, and is the single hardest thing for an incumbent to retrofit.

Why on-prem LLM inference (Ollama / vLLM)

You will hit a brick wall the moment a Tier-1 bank asks "does customer transaction data leave our VPC to reach OpenAI?" The answer must be no, by architecture, not by policy. Open-weight models (Llama 3.x, Qwen, Mistral) running on customer or co-located GPUs are the only durable answer for the next decade.

Why Kubernetes, not "serverless"

Serverless economics fall apart for sustained workloads at fintech volumes (you'll process 50M+ events/day per customer at scale). K8s gives you cloud portability — which is non-negotiable when a Saudi bank requires deployment inside SAMA-approved cloud only, or an Indian regulator asks for data residency proof.

Why Drata / Sprinto from Day 1

SOC 2 and ISO 27001 are not Year-3 problems. Every enterprise prospect will ask in the first call. Automating evidence collection from Month 1 turns a 9-month, ₹40 lakh painful certification into a 4-month, ₹12 lakh routine. This is the highest-ROI tooling decision you'll make in Year 1.

Module 1 · Transaction Monitoring + Sanctions Screening

This is what you ship in Phase 1. Every customer event lands in their Kafka topic, gets enriched against the Neo4j graph (where every entity, device, beneficial owner, and historical counterparty lives), passes through a graph neural net that scores it for layered-fraud / mule / sanctions-evasion patterns, and emits an alert with a structured explainability trace.

  • Detection: Graph-based ML — captures relational patterns rules-engines can't (e.g. fan-in/fan-out, smurfing, identity-rings). Targets: false-positive rate < 30%, recall > 92%.
  • Screening: Real-time sanctions matching against OFAC, UN, EU, MHA, RBI lists with fuzzy + transliteration logic for Arabic/Cyrillic/Devanagari names.
  • Output: Every alert ships with (a) the graph subgraph that triggered it, (b) the model's confidence and feature attributions, (c) a draft FIU/FinCEN-ready narrative.
  • Latency target: p99 < 200ms inline, p99 < 5s post-trade.
  • Throughput target: 50,000 events/sec/customer at peak (= a top-10 Indian fintech).

Module 2 · Continuous KYC / Perpetual KYB

Same graph fabric, different feed. Instead of transactions, the input is identity events: Account Aggregator (India) deltas, sanctions list changes, beneficial-ownership updates from MCA/Companies House, and adverse-media signals scraped from a curated source set.

  • Trigger model: Event-based re-verification — an alert fires when something about the customer changes, not on a calendar.
  • India advantage: Native Account Aggregator + DigiLocker + GSTN integration. This is the moat against US/EU competitors entering India.
  • Workflow: Risk-tiered remediation — low-risk gets auto-cleared, medium gets a step-up verification (selfie + liveness), high gets routed to the customer's compliance team with full evidence pack.

Module 3 · Regulatory Reporting + AI Model Audit

The lakehouse already holds every event, every alert, every decision. Module 3 turns that into regulator-ready outputs and proves to regulators that the AI making those decisions is auditable, unbiased, and reproducible.

  • Reporting: Auto-generated STRs/SARs/CTRs/EFRs in the exact format each regulator wants (FIU-IND XML, FinCEN BSA, MAS STRO, FCA SUP). Customer just reviews + signs.
  • Model audit: Every model decision logged with: input features, model version, confidence, counterfactual ("what would change the outcome"), and bias-test results across protected attributes. This is what the EU AI Act requires from August 2026 — and almost no one has it.
  • Drift detection: Continuous monitoring of model performance vs. baseline, with auto-rollback to previous version if precision degrades > 5%.

Embedded finance is forecast to reach $370B by 2036 (15.8% CAGR). The moat in embedded finance is not the API surface — anyone can build that — it is compliance-as-a-product. Every BaaS / embedded-finance platform that has failed (Synapse, Bond, Galileo's smaller clones) failed because compliance was bolted on as overhead, not built in as architecture. You are uniquely positioned: you already are the compliance architecture.

§ 07 · The Moat

The moat & the final note.

A long-game company has a moat that compounds. Here is what compounds for this one.

i.

The graph compounds.

Every transaction monitored, every entity verified, every alert resolved adds a node and ten edges to the fabric. Your fraud-detection accuracy in Year 5 will be unmatched, and unattainable, by any new entrant.

ii.

The regulator becomes a customer.

Sandbox programs (RBI, DFSA, MAS, FCA) increasingly use vendor RegTech for their own supervision. By Year 4 you should have at least one regulator using your platform. This is the most defensible reference any RegTech company can hold.

iii.

Compliance switching costs.

Once a fintech embeds your transaction monitoring in production, switching means re-certifying with the regulator. The lift is so high that net revenue retention can exceed 140% indefinitely.

iv.

Talent compounds where regulators do.

India and Singapore are now exporting RegTech engineering talent to the world. Building your engineering hub in Bangalore or Pune gives you 10× hiring leverage versus building in San Francisco — and the regulatory experience is on tap.
